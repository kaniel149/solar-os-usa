## 1. The Full Flow — A Solar Install in Florida from Lead to PTO

Total timeline: **4-8 weeks** from signed contract to PTO (Permission to Operate).

### Step 1: Lead Generation (How Installers Get Customers)

Unlike Israel, where the market is smaller and word-of-mouth dominates, the US solar market is a **lead generation machine**. Here's how Florida installers get customers:

| Channel | How It Works | Cost per Lead | Notes |
|---------|-------------|---------------|-------|
| **EnergySage** | Online marketplace — homeowners request quotes, get matched to 3-7 local installers | $50-150 | Highest intent leads. Homeowner is already educated and comparing |
| **Google Ads (PPC)** | Bid on "solar panels Florida," "solar installer near me" etc. | $25-300 | You pay per click. Requires ad expertise or agency |
| **Door-to-Door (D2D)** | Sales reps canvass neighborhoods, knock doors | $100-200 (loaded cost) | Still common in FL. Some cities require solicitation permits. Can damage brand if aggressive |
| **Facebook/Instagram Ads** | Targeted ads to homeowners by zip code, income, home value | $30-100 | Lower intent than Google — homeowner wasn't searching |
| **Referrals** | Happy customers refer friends/family | $0-500 (referral bonus) | Best quality leads. Many companies pay $500-1,000 per referral |
| **Purchased Lead Lists** | Buy lists from lead gen companies | $25-75 | Often non-exclusive (sold to 3-5 companies), can be stale |
| **Home Shows / Events** | Booth at home improvement expos | Varies | Brand awareness + direct leads |
| **Partnerships** | Real estate agents, roofers, electricians refer | Revenue share | Growing channel |

**Key difference from Israel:** In the US, the sales cycle is LONGER. Homeowners shop around, compare 3-5 quotes, and take weeks to decide. EnergySage alone drives millions of comparisons. The competitive pressure is intense.

### Step 2: Sales Appointment

What happens when a rep meets the homeowner:

1. **Energy bill review** — Rep asks for 12 months of utility bills (or pulls usage data from utility portal)
2. **Roof assessment** — Quick visual of roof condition, orientation, shade
3. **Needs analysis** — How much of their bill do they want to offset? Battery backup interest?
4. **Satellite design** — Using software (Aurora Solar, OpenSolar), create a preliminary design on the spot using satellite imagery
5. **Proposal presentation** — Show:
   - System size (kW)
   - Number of panels
   - Estimated annual production (kWh)
   - 25-year savings projection
   - Monthly payment options (cash, loan, lease)
   - Before/after electric bill comparison
6. **Close attempt** — Many companies try to sign same-day ("one-call close")
7. **Florida 3-day right to cancel** — By law, homeowner has 3 business days to cancel any home solicitation contract

### Step 3: Site Survey

After contract signing, a technician does a detailed site survey:

- **Roof measurements** — Exact dimensions, pitch angles (not just satellite estimates)
- **Roof condition assessment** — Age of roof, material type, any damage. In Florida, if a roof is >15 years old, many installers recommend re-roofing first (insurance companies in FL are strict about roof age)
- **Electrical panel inspection** — Main panel amperage (100A, 150A, 200A), available breaker spaces. Many older FL homes have 100A or 150A panels that need an upgrade to 200A ($1,500-3,000 cost)
- **Attic inspection** — Truss type, rafter spacing, structural integrity
- **Shade analysis** — Using tools like SunEye or Solmetric to measure actual shading
- **Utility meter photos** — Document existing meter setup
- **Internet/WiFi check** — For monitoring system connectivity
- **Measurements for conduit runs** — How wiring will route from roof to panel

### Step 4: System Design

The engineering team creates the final design:

- **Software used:** Aurora Solar (dominant), OpenSolar, Helioscope, SolarEdge Designer
- **Design includes:**
  - Panel layout on roof (exact placement, orientation)
  - String sizing / inverter configuration
  - Single-line diagram (electrical schematic)
  - Three-line diagram
  - Equipment specifications
  - Structural engineering calculations (PE-stamped) — **critical in Florida for wind loads**
  - Wind load analysis per ASCE 7-22 / Florida Building Code 2023
- **PE stamp required** — A Florida-licensed Professional Engineer must stamp all structural and electrical plans for permitting

### Step 5: Proposal / Contract

A Florida solar proposal typically includes:

**Proposal Document:**
- System size (e.g., 10.4 kW DC)
- Equipment list (panel brand/model, inverter brand/model, racking)
- Estimated Year 1 production (kWh)
- 25-year production estimate with degradation
- Pricing breakdown:
  - Total system cost (before incentives)
  - Federal ITC credit (30%)
  - Net cost after incentives
  - Monthly loan payment (if financed)
- Savings estimate: current bill vs. solar bill
- ROI / payback period
- Warranties (panel, inverter, workmanship)
- Production guarantee (many companies guarantee 85-90% of projected output)

**Contract Terms (typical):**
- Total price
- Payment schedule (deposit, milestones, or financed)
- Equipment specifications
- Installation timeline commitment
- Warranty terms:
  - Panel manufacturer warranty: 25-30 years
  - Inverter warranty: 12-25 years
  - Workmanship warranty: 10-25 years (varies by installer)
- Production guarantee terms
- Change order process
- Cancellation policy (3-day right + company policy)
- Lien waiver language
- Insurance requirements
- Permitting responsibility (installer handles)

### Step 6: Permitting

**This is one of the biggest differences from Israel.** In Florida, you need a building permit from the local AHJ (Authority Having Jurisdiction — usually the city or county building department).

**New Law — HB 683 (effective July 1, 2025):**
- **5 business day approval** mandate for residential rooftop solar permits statewide
- If the AHJ misses the deadline, the permit is **automatically approved** the next business day
- Supports private inspection providers and virtual inspections
- Supports SolarAPP+ (automated instant permits)

**What gets submitted:**
1. Permit application form (varies by jurisdiction)
2. PE-stamped structural engineering plans
3. PE-stamped electrical plans
4. Single-line diagram
5. Equipment spec sheets (panels, inverters, racking)
6. Site plan / roof layout
7. Wind load calculations
8. Product approvals (NOA — Notice of Acceptance — required in Miami-Dade/Broward HVHZ)

**County-specific notes:**

| County | Pre-HB 683 Timeline | Post-HB 683 | Special Requirements |
|--------|---------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Miami-Dade** | Up to 30 days | 5 business days | HVHZ: NOA + TAS 201/202/203 impact testing required. 175 mph wind design |
| **Broward** | Up to 30 days | 5 business days | HVHZ: Product Control approval + TAS testing. 170 mph wind |
| **Palm Beach** | 1-2 weeks | 5 business days | High wind zone, NOA for some areas |
| **Orange (Orlando)** | ~24 hours (early SolarAPP+ adopter) | 1-2 days | Very progressive, fast approvals |
| **Hillsborough (Tampa)** | 1-2 weeks | 5 business days | Standard FBC requirements |
| **Duval (Jacksonville)** | 1-2 weeks | 5 business days | Standard FBC requirements |

**Permit fees:** Typically $100-500 depending on jurisdiction and system size.

**Key concept — AHJ:** Authority Having Jurisdiction. This is the local government entity that issues building permits and performs inspections. In Israel, this is roughly equivalent to the local municipality building department, but in the US, each city/county has its own rules, fees, and quirks.

### Step 7: HOA Approval

**Yes, HOAs (Homeowners Associations) are a BIG deal in Florida.**

~40-50% of Florida homes are in HOAs. But Florida has one of the strongest solar access laws in the US:

**Florida Statute 163.04 — Solar Rights:**
- HOAs **cannot prohibit** solar panel installation
- HOAs **can** specify roof location, but ONLY within 45° of due south
- Any restriction **must not impair effective operation** of the system
- The law applies retroactively to all HOAs regardless of when bylaws were adopted
- If you win a lawsuit against an HOA blocking solar, they pay your attorney's fees

**In practice:**
- Most installers submit an "Architectural Review" application to the HOA after contract signing
- HOA has 30-45 days to respond (varies by HOA bylaws)
- Most HOAs approve with minor conditions (conduit routing, panel color matching)
- If HOA denies or stalls unreasonably, cite F.S. 163.04 — this usually resolves it
- Smart installers handle HOA paperwork as part of their service

### Step 8: Utility Interconnection Application

This happens in parallel with permitting. The installer submits an interconnection application to the utility company.

**FPL (Florida Power & Light) — Largest utility in FL:**
- Submit via online net metering portal
- Include: signed Interconnection Agreement, system specs, inverter UL 1741 cert
- System must be ≤115% of annual household usage
- No application fee for Tier 1 (<10 kW) — most residential
- FPL reviews for grid safety and capacity
- Timeline: 2-4 weeks for approval

**Duke Energy Florida:**
- Similar online application process
- Follows Florida PSC interconnection rules
- Same size tiers as FPL

**TECO (Tampa Electric):**
- Follows state PSC tiers
- Residential focus on Tier 1 (<10 kW)
- Similar timeline

**Interconnection Tiers (statewide):**
| Tier | System Size | Requirements |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| Tier 1 | ≤10 kW | Minimal — most residential systems |
| Tier 2 | 10-100 kW | Additional review, may need insurance |
| Tier 3 | >100 kW | Full engineering study, commercial |

### Step 9: Installation Day(s)

Typical residential installation: **1-3 days**

**Day 1: Roof Work**
- Install roof attachments / mounting hardware
  - Composition shingle: Standard lag bolts with flashing
  - Tile roof: Tile hooks or comp-out (remove tiles, install comp shingle base, mount rails) — very common in FL
  - Metal roof: Standing seam clamps or through-fasteners
  - Flat roof: Ballasted or mechanically attached tilt-up system
- Install racking / rail system
- Mount solar panels on rails

**Day 2: Electrical Work**
- Install inverter(s) — string inverter (wall-mounted) or microinverters (already on panels)
- Run conduit from roof to electrical panel area
- Install rapid shutdown device(s)
- Wire panels → inverter → main panel
- Install production meter (if required)
- Install monitoring equipment (WiFi gateway)
- If battery: Install battery system (Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ, etc.)

**Day 3 (if needed): Finalize**
- Complete electrical connections
- System testing
- Clean up site

**Who does what:**
- **Solar crew (2-4 people):** Racking, panel mounting, conduit, inverter installation
- **Licensed electrician:** Final electrical connections, panel work, inspections
- **Roofer (if needed):** Roof repairs, tile manipulation, re-roofing sections
- **PE / Engineer:** Does NOT come to site — designs remotely, stamps plans

**In Florida, the licensed Solar Contractor (CV license) oversees the whole project.** Subs handle specific trades under their supervision.

### Step 10: Inspections

Two separate inspections are required:

**1. Building Department (AHJ) Inspection:**
- Inspector comes to the property
- Verifies installation matches approved plans
- Checks electrical connections, grounding, rapid shutdown
- Checks structural attachments, roof penetrations
- Checks labeling and safety signage
- Pass → inspector signs off on permit

**2. Utility Inspection (if required):**
- Some utilities (like FPL) do their own inspection or just require photos
- Verify meter setup, disconnect switch, anti-islanding
- May swap meter to bi-directional net meter

**Virtual inspections** are now allowed under HB 683 (2025) — inspector reviews photos/video remotely.

### Step 11: PTO — Permission to Operate

**PTO is the finish line.** This is when the utility formally authorizes the system to operate and export power to the grid.

**What PTO means:**
- The system is legally allowed to generate and export electricity
- Net metering begins — the meter runs both ways
- The homeowner starts saving money

**Timeline to PTO after inspection:**
- FPL: 1-2 weeks typically (meter swap + final approval)
- Duke: 1-3 weeks
- TECO: 1-2 weeks

**IMPORTANT:** The customer CANNOT turn on the system before PTO. Operating before PTO can void interconnection agreements and potentially damage equipment (no anti-islanding protection during outages).

### Step 12: Monitoring Handoff

After PTO, the installer:
1. Activates the monitoring platform (Enphase Enlighten, SolarEdge, Tesla app, etc.)
2. Shows the homeowner how to use the app
3. Explains what to look for (production, alerts, outages)
4. Sets up email/phone alerts for system issues
5. Provides warranty documentation
6. Schedules any follow-up check (30-day, 90-day)

### Net Metering — Current Status (Critical!)

**Current law: 1:1 net metering at full retail rate.**

- Excess solar production credits your bill at the SAME rate you pay for electricity
- Credits roll over month to month
- At end of billing year, excess credits are paid out at avoided cost rate (~$0.03-0.04/kWh)
- System size capped at 115% of annual household usage
- No community solar allowed by law

**Political risk:**
- In 2022, the Florida legislature passed **HB-741** which would have gutted net metering (reduced credits to 75% by 2024, then lower)
- Governor DeSantis **vetoed it** citing inflation concerns
- The utility lobby (especially FPL/NextEra) continues to push for net metering reform
- **This is an ongoing risk** — the economics of FL solar depend heavily on 1:1 net metering
- No changes enacted as of early 2026, but watch legislative sessions closely

### Tax Exemptions

**Sales Tax Exemption:**
- Florida's 6% sales tax is **fully exempt** for solar energy systems
- Covers panels, inverters, racking, batteries, solar water heaters
- Made permanent in 2005
- Installer handles the exemption — customer doesn't pay it

**Property Tax Exemption:**
- **100% exemption** for residential solar systems
- Solar panels increase home value but do NOT increase property taxes
- 80% abatement for commercial properties
- Implemented by local property appraiser

### Hurricane Requirements

**This is THE defining factor of Florida solar vs. anywhere else.** Florida is the most hurricane-exposed solar market in the US.

**Florida Building Code (FBC) 8th Edition, 2023:**
- Based on ASCE 7-22 wind load calculations
- Design wind speeds vary by location:

| Zone | Design Wind Speed | Counties |
|------|------------------|----------|
| HVHZ (High Velocity Hurricane Zone) | **175 mph** | Miami-Dade |
| HVHZ | **170 mph** | Broward |
| Coastal High Wind | **160-170 mph** | Palm Beach, Collier, Lee, Monroe |
| Inland High Wind | **140-160 mph** | Most of central/north FL |
| Standard | **120-140 mph** | Parts of north FL |

**What this means for solar installations:**
- Every system needs PE-stamped wind load calculations
- Mounting systems must be rated for the specific wind zone
- In HVHZ (Miami-Dade/Broward): Products need **NOA (Notice of Acceptance)** and must pass **TAS 201/202/203** impact testing
- Roof attachments must handle both uplift AND shear forces
- Exposure Category C (open terrain) typically required for coastal sites
- Some racking manufacturers offer "hurricane-rated" systems specifically for FL

### Roof Types in Florida

| Roof Type | Prevalence in FL | Solar Mounting Method | Challenges |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------------|------------|
| **Asphalt Shingle** | ~40% | Standard lag bolts + flashing | Simplest install. Watch for older shingles |
| **Concrete/Clay Tile** | ~35% | Tile hooks, comp-out method, or tile replacement hooks | Most common headache. Tiles break easily. Comp-out = remove tiles, install comp shingle, mount on that |
| **Metal (Standing Seam)** | ~10% | Clamps (no roof penetrations!) | Easiest and cleanest install |
| **Flat / Low Slope** | ~10% | Ballasted racking or mechanically attached tilt-up | Common on commercial, some modern homes |
| **Barrel Tile** | ~5% | Similar to concrete tile — specialized hooks | Expensive, tricky |

**Tile roofs are the #1 installation challenge in Florida.** Many Israeli installers are used to flat concrete roofs. In FL, you'll deal with tile constantly.

### Insurance Considerations

- Homeowners insurance typically covers solar panels as part of the structure
- Some insurers charge a **small premium increase** ($10-30/month) for solar
- Florida's insurance market is already stressed (hurricane risk) — adding solar can occasionally cause issues with smaller insurers
- **Roof age matters:** If the roof is >15 years, many FL insurers won't insure the home regardless of solar. This means the homeowner may need a new roof BEFORE solar
- Solar installations should NOT void existing roof warranties if done properly
- Installers should carry their own liability insurance ($1M minimum for Tier 2)

### HOA Solar Access Law (F.S. 163.04)

Covered in detail in Step 7 above. Key points:
- **Cannot prohibit solar installation**
- Can restrict placement within 45° of due south only
- Restrictions cannot impair system performance
- Attorney's fees to prevailing party in disputes
- Applies to all HOAs retroactively

## 3. Licensing & Legal

### Required License: Certified Solar Contractor (CV)

**In Florida, there is a SPECIFIC solar contractor license.** This is different from Israel where solar might fall under general electrical contracting.

**License type: CV (Certified Solar Contractor)**
- Issued by: Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR)
- Scope: Install, repair, maintain photovoltaic (PV) systems, solar pool/spa heating, domestic solar water heating
- **Statewide** — valid in all 67 Florida counties

**There is NO "EC13" solar-specific electrical license.** The solar contractor license is the CV.

**Requirements to obtain:**
| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Age | 18+ |
| Background check | Criminal + fingerprints |
| Experience | **4 years total** (including 1 year as foreman/supervisor) in PV and solar thermal |
| Education alternatives | 4-year construction degree = 3 years credit + 1 year experience needed. College credits can sub up to 3 years |
| Exams | Two exams: (1) Technical/Safety (solar tech, codes, permitting, safety) (2) Business & Finance |
| General Liability Insurance | $100,000 minimum |
| Property Damage Insurance | $25,000 minimum |
| Workers' Compensation | Required (or exemption if no employees) |
| Financial Stability | Credit check; if FICO <660, must provide surety bond |

**Who does NOT need the license:**
- Employees working under a CV-licensed solar contractor
- (But someone in the company must hold the CV license)

### Can Other Trades Install Solar?

| License Type | Can Install Solar? | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|
| **EC (Certified Electrical Contractor)** | **Partial** — can do electrical integration but NOT full solar panel installation | May handle solar+storage electrical work |
| **General Contractor (CGC)** | **No** — cannot perform solar installations | Solar is a specialty trade |
| **Roofing Contractor (CCC)** | **No** — cannot install solar panels | Can be subcontracted for roof work only |
| **Registered Solar Contractor (RSC/CW)** | **Limited** — only in specific local jurisdictions, not statewide | CW = residential solar water heating only |

**Bottom line:** You need the **CV license** to run a solar company in Florida.

### Building Permit Requirements by County

All counties now follow HB 683 (5-day approval mandate). What varies:

| County | Notable Requirements | Fees (approx) |

| **Broward** | HVHZ: Product Control approval required. TAS testing. 170 mph | $200-400 |
| **Palm Beach** | High wind zone. Some areas require NOA | $150-350 |
| **Orange (Orlando)** | Early SolarAPP+ adopter. Very fast — often 24-48 hours | $100-250 |
| **Hillsborough (Tampa)** | Standard FBC. Reasonable process | $100-300 |
| **Duval (Jacksonville)** | Standard FBC. Growing solar market | $100-300 |

### NABCEP Certification

**Not legally required in Florida, but strongly recommended.**

- **NABCEP = North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners**
- The gold standard certification for solar professionals
- Types: PV Installation Professional (PVIP), PV Design Specialist, PV Technical Sales
- Benefits:
  - Instant credibility with customers
  - Required by some commercial/utility projects and government bids
  - Some financing companies and lead gen platforms prefer NABCEP-certified installers
  - Continuing education keeps you current
- **For Kaniel's business:** NABCEP certification would be a strong differentiator, especially when competing against the big national chains

### Insurance Requirements

| Type | Requirement | Minimum |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Workers' Compensation** | Required if you have ANY employees (even 1 part-time) | State minimums per payroll |
| **Workers' Comp Exemption** | Available for sole proprietors, partners, LLC members, or corporate officers owning ≥10% (max 3 per company) | File with state |
| **General Liability** | Required for license | $100,000 minimum (most carry $1M+) |
| **Property Damage** | Required for license | $25,000 minimum |
| **Commercial Auto** | Recommended for service vehicles | Varies |
| **Umbrella Policy** | Recommended | $1-2M |

**Subcontractor warning:** You MUST verify subcontractors' workers' comp coverage BEFORE they start work. If an uninsured sub gets hurt, YOU are liable.

### Lien Laws (Chapter 713, Florida Statutes)

Florida has **contractor's lien laws** (also called mechanics' lien):
- If you complete solar work and the homeowner doesn't pay, you can file a lien on the property
- You MUST serve a **Notice to Owner** within 45 days of first work on the project
- Lien must be filed within 90 days of final work
- Lawsuit to enforce must be filed within 1 year of the lien recording
- **For homeowners:** They can demand a Contractor's Final Affidavit and Lien Waiver before final payment — this is standard in solar contracts

### Inverters: Enphase vs SolarEdge in Florida

| Feature | Enphase (Microinverters) | SolarEdge (String + Optimizers) |
|---------|-------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Type** | One microinverter per panel | One central inverter + optimizer per panel |
| **Florida popularity** | **Growing fast** — now ~50%+ of residential | **Strong** — ~40% of residential |
| **Shade handling** | Excellent — each panel independent | Good — optimizers help, but string-level limits |
| **Rapid shutdown** | **Built-in** — inherent compliance | Requires additional RSD components |
| **Monitoring** | Panel-level via Enlighten app | Panel-level via mySolarEdge app |
| **Warranty** | 25 years | 12 years (extendable to 25) |
| **Failure mode** | One micro fails = one panel offline | Central inverter fails = whole system down |
| **Cost** | Slightly higher | Slightly lower |
| **Battery integration** | Enphase IQ Battery (3.36/10.08 kWh) | SolarEdge Energy Hub + compatible batteries |
| **Best for FL** | Ideal for complex roofs, shade, hurricane resilience (no single point of failure) | Good for simple roofs, cost-conscious |

**Trend:** Enphase is winning in Florida due to:
1. Built-in rapid shutdown compliance (NEC 2020)
2. No single point of failure (hurricane-relevant)
3. Easier installation on complex FL roof shapes
4. Strong battery integration

### Wind Load Requirements (Detailed)

**Florida Building Code 2023 + ASCE 7-22:**

- Wind load formula: `q_z = 0.00256 × K_z × K_zt × K_d × K_e × V²`
- V = basic wind speed for the location (120-175 mph)
- Exposure Category C typically for Florida (open terrain, flat)
- Solar arrays add load to the roof — must not exceed roof's structural capacity
- **Corner and edge zones** have HIGHER wind loads than center of roof — panel placement matters
- GCr (pressure coefficients) up to 1.9 for uplift

**In HVHZ (Miami-Dade, Broward):**
- Products need NOA (Notice of Acceptance)
- Must pass TAS 201 (large missile impact), TAS 202 (uniform static air pressure), TAS 203 (cyclic wind)
- This limits which racking systems can be used — not all national brands are approved

### Fire Code Setbacks

**NEC 2020 + Florida amendments:**
- **Residential:** Access pathways required on roof for firefighter access
- Typical setback: **3 feet** from ridge line
- **3-foot clear pathway** on at least one side of roof
- Some AHJs require pathways on all sides
- Fire department access considerations
- Check local AHJ — requirements vary

### Rapid Shutdown Requirements

**NEC 2020 Section 690.12 (adopted in Florida):**
- All PV system conductors on/within buildings must reduce to **≤80 volts within 30 seconds** of shutdown
- Single rapid shutdown switch required per system — clearly labeled, readily accessible
- Must use UL-listed PV Hazard Control System (PVHCS) components per UL 3741
- **Compliance methods:**
  - Microinverters (Enphase) — inherently compliant (no high DC voltage when AC disconnected)
  - Power optimizers (SolarEdge) — reduce voltage to ~1V per module
  - Dedicated RSD devices (Tigo, APsystems)

## 5. Financing

### How Florida Homeowners Pay

| Method | Market Share (est.) | How It Works |
|--------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Solar Loan** | **~60-65%** | Third-party lender finances the system. Homeowner owns system, claims ITC |
| **Cash Purchase** | **~20-25%** | Homeowner pays outright. Lowest total cost. Claims ITC |
| **Lease** | **~5-10%** | Third party owns system, homeowner pays monthly. No ITC for homeowner |
| **PPA (Power Purchase Agreement)** | **~5-10%** | Third party owns system, homeowner pays per kWh generated. No ITC for homeowner |

**Loans dominate in Florida.** Unlike California where leases/PPAs were historically popular, Florida homeowners prefer ownership (and the ITC benefit).

### Major Solar Lenders in Florida

| Lender | Typical Terms | Dealer Fee | Notes |
|--------|-------------|------------|-------|
| **GoodLeap** | 10-25 year terms, 2.99-8.99% APR | **15-37%** (avg ~19%) | Largest solar lender. Easy approval. HIGH dealer fees |
| **Mosaic** | 10-25 years, 3.99-8.99% APR | **12-25%** (avg ~18%) | Second largest. Similar to GoodLeap |
| **Sunlight Financial** | 10-25 years, 3.49-7.99% APR | **15-30%** | Had financial troubles in 2023-2024, restructured |
| **Dividend Finance** | 10-25 years, 2.99-7.99% APR | **15-25%** | Growing market share |
| **Credit Unions / Banks** | 5-20 years, 5-10% APR | **0%** (no dealer fee) | Best total cost for homeowner, but harder to sell (higher monthly payment) |

### Understanding Dealer Fees (CRITICAL for Installers)

**This is one of the most important concepts in US solar financing.** It doesn't exist in Israel.

**How it works:**
1. Homeowner wants to buy a $30,000 solar system
2. Installer offers a "25-year, 1.49% APR" loan through GoodLeap
3. That low interest rate is subsidized by a **dealer fee** — say 25%
4. GoodLeap actually funds $30,000 to the installer
5. But the homeowner's loan balance is **$37,500** ($30,000 + 25% dealer fee = $7,500 added)
6. The homeowner pays interest on $37,500 — not $30,000
7. The installer receives $30,000 (the actual system cost)

**Why this matters:**
- Homeowners often don't realize the loan is inflated
- This has become a **major regulatory issue** — lawsuits in multiple states
- Minnesota AG sued GoodLeap, Mosaic, Sunlight, and Dividend in 2024 over dealer fee disclosure
- CFPB (Consumer Financial Protection Bureau) published a spotlight report in 2024
- Expect increased regulation and transparency requirements

**For honest installers:** This is an opportunity to differentiate. Be transparent about pricing.

**Typical fee structures:**

| Loan Product | APR | Typical Dealer Fee | Net Effect |
|-------------|-----|-------------------|-----------|
| 25-year, 0.99% | Very low | 25-37% | Homeowner pays WAY more total |
| 25-year, 2.99% | Low | 18-25% | Still significant markup |
| 25-year, 5.99% | Mid | 10-15% | More reasonable |
| 25-year, 8.99% | Market rate | 0-5% | Most honest for consumer |
| 10-year, market rate | 6-9% | 0-5% | Best total cost after cash |

### Federal ITC (Investment Tax Credit) — 30%

**This is the #1 incentive that makes solar viable in Florida (no state incentive!).**

**How it works:**
- Homeowner installs solar and pays for it (cash or loan — NOT lease/PPA)
- At tax time, they file **IRS Form 5695** (Residential Energy Credits)
- They get a **tax credit** equal to 30% of total qualified costs
- This is a CREDIT (reduces tax liability dollar-for-dollar), NOT a deduction
- Covers: panels, inverters, batteries (≥3 kWh), racking, wiring, installation labor, permit fees
- **Non-refundable** — you must owe federal taxes to use it
- If credit exceeds tax liability, remainder carries forward to future tax years

**Example:**
- System cost: $30,000
- ITC: 30% × $30,000 = **$9,000 tax credit**
- If homeowner owes $7,000 in federal taxes, they pay $0 and carry $2,000 to next year

**CRITICAL UPDATE (2025):** The "One Big Beautiful Bill" (July 2025) eliminated the residential solar ITC for systems placed in service after December 31, 2025. **For new installations in 2026+, the 30% ITC for homeowners is GONE for cash/loan purchases.** Commercial ITC under Section 48E still exists for leases/PPAs where a company owns the system.

**This changes the game dramatically for residential solar sales in 2026.**

### No State Solar Incentive — How Do Installers Sell?

Florida has **NO state solar rebate or incentive.** This is unusual — many states (California, New York, Massachusetts) have additional state incentives.

**How FL installers sell without state incentives:**
1. **Federal ITC (30%)** — the main financial driver (through 2025!)
2. **Sales tax exemption** — saves ~6% off system cost
3. **Property tax exemption** — home value goes up, taxes don't
4. **Net metering (1:1)** — strong savings on monthly bill
5. **High electricity costs** — FL rates are above national average
6. **Hurricane resilience** — battery backup as protection against outages
7. **Electricity rate increases** — "Lock in your rate" messaging
8. **Environmental messaging** — works with some demographics

### PACE Financing (Property Assessed Clean Energy)

**Available in Florida** through programs like Ygrene, PACE Funding Group, Florida PACE.

**How PACE works:**
- 100% financing, no down payment
- Repayment through **property tax bill** (not a personal loan)
- Terms: 5-25 years
- Tied to the **property**, not the homeowner — transfers with sale
- No credit score requirement (based on property equity)

**Controversy:**
- PACE has been heavily criticized and regulated
- Higher effective interest rates than traditional loans
- Property tax lien can complicate home sales and mortgage refinancing
- Some mortgage lenders won't approve homes with PACE liens
- Florida has added consumer protections but it remains controversial
- Many mainstream solar companies avoid PACE

### SELF (Solar and Energy Loan Fund)

- **Nonprofit** lender serving underserved Florida homeowners
- Rates: 5-9.5% (promotions as low as 6.99%)
- Terms: 3-10 years
- Loan amounts: $3,000-$25,000
- No minimum credit score — proof of responsible property ownership
- Zero down payment
- Good for customers who can't qualify for traditional solar loans
