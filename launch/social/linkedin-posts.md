## Post 2: The Tool Consolidation Problem

Solar installers are paying $300-500/month for software.

Here's a typical stack:
→ CRM: $65/mo
→ Proposal tool: $89/mo
→ Project management: $49/mo
→ Invoicing: $35/mo
→ Scheduling: $25/mo
→ Lead tracking: $50/mo

That's 6 logins. 6 dashboards. Zero integration.

When a lead comes in, someone manually enters it into the CRM.
Then copies it to the proposal tool.
Then creates a project card.
Then sends an invoice from a different system.

Every handoff is a chance to lose data — or lose the customer.

Solar OS replaces all of it. One platform. One login. $49/month.

I built it because I lived this exact pain for years
running my own solar company. Every feature exists because
I needed it in the field.

If your team spends more time in software than on rooftops,
something is broken. We're fixing it.

## Post 5: Customer Discovery

Before writing a single line of code for the US market,
I spent 3 weeks just talking to solar installers.

Not selling. Listening.

Here's what I heard over and over:

"I lose track of leads because they're in 4 different places."

"My proposal process takes 2 hours per customer."

"I don't know which jobs are profitable until months later."

"I hired an office manager just to manage our software."

Every conversation confirmed the same thing —
the pain isn't lack of tools. It's too many tools that don't talk to each other.

Solar installers don't want more features.
They want fewer logins.

That's the design principle behind Solar OS.
Every feature lives in one ecosystem.
Lead comes in → proposal goes out → project kicks off → invoice sends → done.

No exports. No imports. No copy-paste.

If you're building a product, talk to 20 customers before writing code.

## Post 8: What Solar Installers Actually Need

I've sold solar for years. Here's what a CRM built by tech people gets wrong:

They optimize for "pipeline visibility."
Installers need to know: "Which roof am I on tomorrow?"

They build complex automation workflows.
Installers need: "Send this proposal before the customer calls someone else."

They add 50 custom fields.
Installers need: "Name, address, system size, close date."

The gap between enterprise software and field reality is massive.

Solar OS is built from the field up:
→ Mobile-first (because you're on a roof, not at a desk)
→ Fast proposals (minutes, not hours)
→ Job tracking that matches how crews actually work
→ Simple invoicing tied to project milestones

No training videos. No onboarding calls.
If you can use a phone, you can use Solar OS.

That's not a limitation. That's the product.

## Post 9: The Bootstrapper's Advantage

No investors. No board. No runway pressure.

Solar OS is 100% bootstrapped. Here's what that means:

→ I ship features customers ask for, not features investors want to see
