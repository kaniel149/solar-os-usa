# Solar Lead Management: From First Call to Signed Contract

Every solar installer has the same complaint: "I'm getting leads, but I'm not closing enough of them."

The problem usually isn't lead quality. It's lead management. Leads come in from web forms, phone calls, referrals, and door-knocking — and then they sit. They sit while you're on an install. They sit over the weekend. They sit because nobody remembered to follow up after the proposal.

In residential solar, **speed and persistence are the two biggest factors in close rate.** The installer who responds first and follows up consistently wins — even over the installer with the lower price.

This guide covers the complete lead management process for solar installers, from the moment a lead comes in to the day they sign the contract.

## Stage 1: Lead Capture and Response

The clock starts ticking the moment someone expresses interest. Every hour you wait to respond reduces your probability of closing that lead.

### The Five-Minute Rule

Research consistently shows that contacting a lead within five minutes of their inquiry makes you **21 times more likely to qualify that lead** compared to waiting 30 minutes. In solar, where homeowners often request quotes from 3–5 installers simultaneously, the first responder advantage is massive.

### Set Up Automated Instant Response

When a lead submits a web form or fills out a request:

1. **Immediate auto-text:** "Hi [Name], thanks for your interest in solar! I'm [Your Name] from [Company]. I'll call you within the next few minutes to answer your questions."
2. **Immediate auto-email:** Brief confirmation with your company info, a one-line value proposition, and your direct phone number
3. **CRM notification:** Alert to the assigned sales rep's phone

The auto-response buys you time while establishing that you're responsive and professional.

### Lead Source Tracking

Every lead should be tagged with its source from day one:

- **Website form** — Which page? Which campaign drove them there?
- **Phone call** — Were they responding to an ad, a referral, or a Google search?
- **Referral** — Who referred them? (Track this — you'll want to thank and incentivize referrers)
- **Door knock** — Which neighborhood? Which canvasser?
- **Home show / event** — Which event?
- **Purchased lead** — Which provider? (Track cost per lead and close rate by provider)

This data is what tells you where to spend your marketing dollars. Without it, you're guessing.

## Stage 2: Qualification

Not every lead is a good lead. Qualifying early saves you from wasting time on site visits that will never close.

### The Five Qualification Questions

1. **Do you own your home?** — Renters can't install solar. This eliminates non-starters immediately.
2. **What's your average monthly electric bill?** — Under $80/month rarely makes financial sense for solar. Over $150/month is your sweet spot.
3. **What's your roof situation?** — Age, condition, shade. A roof that needs replacement in 2 years is a delay, not a disqualifier — you can partner with a roofer.
4. **What's your timeline?** — "Researching for next year" is different from "I want to start saving now." Prioritize accordingly.
5. **How's your credit?** — If they're financing, a quick credit conversation early prevents surprises later. Frame it gently: "Most of our customers finance — are you comfortable with a credit check when we get to that stage?"

### Lead Scoring

Assign a simple score based on qualification:

- **Hot (8–10):** Homeowner, $150+ electric bill, good roof, ready now, strong credit
- **Warm (5–7):** Homeowner, moderate bill, some timeline flexibility, minor roof questions
- **Cool (1–4):** Renter, low bill, distant timeline, significant barriers

Focus your time and fastest follow-up on hot leads. Warm leads get nurtured. Cool leads go into a long-term drip sequence.

## Stage 3: Site Visit and Assessment

The site visit is where you shift from salesperson to trusted advisor. It's also where most installers either build or lose trust.

### Before the Visit

- **Confirm the appointment** — Text reminder 24 hours before and 2 hours before
- **Research the property** — Pull satellite imagery, check roof orientation and shading
- **Prepare preliminary design** — Have a rough system layout ready to show the homeowner
- **Review their electric bill** — If they sent it ahead of time, know their usage patterns

### During the Visit

- **Walk the property** — Check roof condition, electrical panel capacity, shade sources, and mounting options
- **Take photos** — Roof, electrical panel, meter, any shade obstructions. These go into your project file.
- **Listen more than you talk** — Understand their motivation (savings, environment, energy independence, home value)
- **Set expectations** — "I'll have a custom proposal for you within 24 hours"

### After the Visit

- **Send a thank-you text** within 2 hours: "Great meeting you today, [Name]. Working on your custom proposal now — you'll have it by [time tomorrow]."
- **Update your CRM** immediately with site visit notes, photos, and any technical details
- **Begin proposal preparation** — same day

## Stage 4: Proposal Delivery and Follow-Up

The proposal is your most important sales document. How you deliver it and what you do after matters as much as what's in it.

### Delivery Best Practices

- **Speed:** Deliver within 24 hours of the site visit. 48 hours maximum. Longer than that and you're already losing momentum.
- **Method:** Email the proposal, then follow up with a call to walk through it. Don't just send and hope.
- **Personalization:** Reference specific details from your conversation. "You mentioned wanting backup power for hurricane season — I've included a battery storage option on page 2."

### The Follow-Up Sequence

Most solar deals don't close on the first proposal review. Here's the follow-up cadence that keeps deals alive without being annoying:

- **Day 0:** Proposal sent + phone call walkthrough
- **Day 2:** Text message — "Had a chance to review the proposal? Happy to answer any questions."
- **Day 5:** Email with additional value — a relevant article, updated incentive info, or a customer testimonial
- **Day 7:** Phone call — "Checking in on the proposal. Any questions or concerns I can address?"
- **Day 14:** Final follow-up — "Still interested in solar? I'd love to help you lock in the current incentives before they change."
- **Day 21+:** Move to monthly nurture drip if no response

### Handling Objections

The most common objections and how to address them:

**"I need to think about it"** — "Totally understand. What specific concerns are you weighing? I might be able to provide some information that helps."

**"I'm getting other quotes"** — "Smart move. When you're comparing, make sure you're looking at total cost of ownership — some quotes leave out monitoring or permit fees. I'm happy to help you compare apples to apples."

**"It's too expensive"** — Revisit financing options. Reframe: "With financing, your monthly solar payment is likely less than your current electric bill — you're saving from month one."

**"I'm worried about my roof"** — "We can coordinate with a roofing partner. Some homeowners do a roof and solar together to lock in both investments."

## Stage 5: Contract and Close

When the homeowner is ready to move forward, make signing frictionless.

### Closing Best Practices

- **Use e-signatures** — Don't make them print, sign, and scan. Digital signing should take under 5 minutes.
- **Walk through the contract** — Don't just email it. Review key terms on a call or in person.
- **Collect the deposit immediately** — Online payment processing. Don't let "I'll mail a check" delay the start.
- **Set expectations for next steps** — "You'll hear from our project coordinator within 48 hours to schedule your installation."

### Post-Signing Communication

The period between contract signing and installation is where buyer's remorse lives. Keep the homeowner informed and confident:

- **Day 1:** Welcome email with timeline and what to expect
- **Permit submission:** "Your permit has been submitted to [County]. We'll update you when it's approved."
- **Permit approval:** "Great news — permit approved! Scheduling your installation now."
- **Pre-installation:** "Your installation is scheduled for [date]. Here's what to expect."

## Measuring Your Lead Management Performance

Track these metrics monthly:

- **Lead response time** — Average time from inquiry to first contact (target: under 5 minutes for web leads)
- **Qualification rate** — Percentage of leads that pass initial qualification (healthy range: 40–60%)
- **Site visit rate** — Percentage of qualified leads that schedule a site visit (target: 60%+)
- **Proposal delivery time** — Average hours from site visit to proposal sent (target: under 24 hours)
- **Close rate** — Percentage of proposals that become signed contracts (healthy range: 25–40%)
- **Sales cycle length** — Average days from first contact to signed contract (typical: 14–45 days)

## Key Takeaways

- **Speed wins** — respond to new leads within 5 minutes and deliver proposals within 24 hours
- **Qualify early** — five simple questions prevent wasted site visits
- **Follow up persistently** — most deals close after the second or third contact, not the first
- **Track everything** — lead source, response time, and close rate by source tell you where to invest
- **Automate the routine** — auto-responses, follow-up reminders, and status updates should happen without manual effort
- **Keep communicating after signing** — the period between contract and installation is when buyer's remorse strikes
